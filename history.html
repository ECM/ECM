History

Our History

Development and application of numerical models to address estuarine and coastal hydrodynamic and water quality problems were initiated in the mid to late 1970s in the US. Model development and applications continued to increase in both scope and complexity with the rapid evolution of computational platforms and software. By the mid-1980s, it was common to have one or two sessions (5 to 10 presentations) at a technical conference devoted to this topic area. Sensing a growing interest and the development of a critical mass of active participants in the field, in 1989 Dr. Malcolm L. Spaulding, then Assistant Professor, Ocean Engineering, University of RI (URI) proposed to the Coastal Zone Management Committee of the Waterways, Port, Coastal and Ocean Division, American Society of Civil Engineers to hold a specialty conference, Estuarine and Coastal Modeling, to meet the need for a forum where modelers could present and discuss the technical aspects of modeling with their peers. Members of the first organizing committee included Alan Blumberg, Ralph Cheng, Peter Sheng, and Craig Swanson. The success of the first conference led to subsequent biennial conferences, up to the present. The conference was initially organized under the ASCE umbrella through ECM5, at which point the conference became self-sustaining with Joe Pittle, University of RI, Conference Center handling conference management. The conference has typically been directed by a five to seven member organizing committee chaired by Malcolm Spaulding (ECM1 to ECM12) and Rich Signell (ECM13 â€“ present), with a much larger advisory committee. Organizing and advisory committee members represent the breadth of expertise and constituencies in the field and has evolved as the conference has matured. The conference locations were purposely moved around the US to ensure the widest possible access to participants, with a particular emphasis on attracting the next generation of professionals in the field. Conferences are typically held in retreat-like setting with a maximum of about 125 people to encourage interaction and help strengthen ties between modeling communities. A wide range of modeling issues are encouraged, including advances in physical understanding, numerical algorithm development, model applications, and better tools. A wide range of modeling topics are encouraged as well, including storm surge, eutrophication, larval transport, search and rescue, oil spills, fisheries issues, coastal erosion and contaminated sediment transport. Many conferences also have special themes.
